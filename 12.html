A Green Legacy For All

Local people are rarely given the opportunity to contribute to the current amenities of their community and to help establish its legacy for future generations.

This is one of those rare opportunities.

The historic towns of Braintree and Bocking and their residents have long been fortunate not only to be part of local and national history, but also to have been the recipients of civic generosity. In particular, this has come from a variety of sources, notably the Courtauld family.

In 1888, Sydney Courtauld donated what had been the gardens of his Bocking Place home to provide, free of charge, a tranquil green space wherein children could play safely and local residents could enjoy the peace and quiet of a unique Victorian garden.


In the five acres there are over 250 trees. Native species nestle beside the rare and special Blue Atlas Cedar, three Deodars, a Wellingtonia, and a Lucombe Oak. Recent plantings include a Wollemi Pine and several other new varieties. A mixture of formal and mixed flower beds, shrubs and other plants, carefully tended by one full-time and one part-time gardener, give the gardens their resplendent colour and variety throughout the year.

The open, tree-bordered green is capped at one end by the recently-refurbished children's playground and at the other by the two good-quality tennis courts – which are available for hire. The round pool, the “wild” garden and shady laurel walk add to the enjoyment of our garden visitors. Unique to the gardens is the John Ray Garden, created and maintained by the John Ray Trust to mark the 300th anniversary of the birth of the famous local philosopher and “Father of English Botany”.

The War Memorial plays host to the annual local Armistice Day service, while the historic thatched bandstand allows Bocking Concert Brass and others to continue the long tradition of providing musical entertainment for visitors on a regular basis.

Overall, the Gardens provide a unique and varied facility for local residents today and an irreplaceable green legacy for their future.


But there is a problem.

The Trust Fund set up by the Courtaulds in 1888 is administered by the Board of Trustees. As a private trust, the Gardens need regular funds to maintain their beauty and to keep the gates open for all visitors. Regular events, sponsorship, Braintree District Council and various fund-raising activities contribute to this, but the Friends of the Public Gardens are the bedrock of their support.

The effects of the current recession and recent considerable expenditure on the gardener's cottage, drainage and essential maintenance have severely depleted the Trust's finances.

Our Fund-raising Committee is working on events and ideas to help, but we need more financial support urgently. That's where you can help.

You can play your part in preserving this historic green space for the community and visitors to the area by joining the Friends, established in 1996, for a small annual fee. Members of the Friends of the Public Gardens receive early notification of all Public Gardens events and an annual newsletter.


Membership is just £15 annually per person or £25 for a couple at the same address.
